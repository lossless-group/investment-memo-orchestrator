# Alpha Partners: 7 Cs Investment Scorecard

**Framework for Evaluating Growth-Stage Direct Investments**

Version 1.0.0 | Last Updated: March 2026

---

## Overview

The 7 Cs framework provides a systematic lens for evaluating growth-stage direct investments across seven dimensions that collectively predict outsized venture returns. Each "C" captures a critical investment criterion — from syndicate quality to return probability — enabling rigorous, repeatable evaluation.

### Scoring Scale

| Score | Label | Percentile |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Exceptional | Top 5% |
| 4 | Above Average | Top 10-25% |
| 3 | Average | Top 50% |
| 2 | Below Average | Bottom 50% |
| 1 | Weak / Disqualifying | Bottom 25% |

### Decision Thresholds

| Recommendation | Criteria |
|----------------|----------|
| **COMMIT** | No dimension below 3, average above 3.5, at least two dimensions at 5 |
| **CONSIDER** | No dimension below 2, average between 2.5 and 3.5 |
| **PASS** | Any dimension scoring 1, or average below 2.5 |

### Dimension Groups

The 7 dimensions are organized into four groups, each placed strategically within the investment memo:

1. **Capital Quality** (2 dimensions) — Capital Syndicate + Capital Efficiency
2. **Market Dominance** (2 dimensions) — Category Leadership + Colossal Market Size
3. **Growth Durability** (2 dimensions) — CAGR + Counter Cyclicality
4. **Return Profile** (1 dimension) — Cash on Cash Return Probability

---

## C1: Capital Syndicate

**What it measures**: Quality, signal strength, and strategic value of the investor syndicate

> The composition of a company's investor base is both a signal and a lever. A strong capital syndicate de-risks execution by providing follow-on capacity, strategic introductions, and institutional credibility. Conversely, a weak or misaligned syndicate can constrain future fundraising, create governance friction, and signal a lack of conviction from sophisticated capital allocators.

**Questions to Answer**:
- Who is the lead investor? What is their track record in this sector?
- What signal does the co-investor group send to future investors?
- What is the total follow-on capacity from existing investors?
- Which investors provide strategic value beyond capital?
- Are there any red flags: tourist investors, weak lead, misaligned incentives?
- How does this syndicate compare to best-in-class at this stage?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Lead investor portfolio and exits in sector
- Co-investor fund sizes and follow-on history
- Prior round investors and their follow-on stance
- Reference calls with portfolio companies of lead investor
- Crunchbase/PitchBook investor profiles

**Red Flags**:
- No clear lead investor or lead has no sector expertise
- Syndicate is primarily angel-driven at growth stage
- Previous investors not following on (negative signal)
- Tourist investors from unrelated sectors
- Investors known for adversarial governance behavior
- Insufficient follow-on reserves for next 2-3 rounds

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Tier-1 lead with deep sector expertise and strong exits; blue-chip co-investors with follow-on reserves; prior investors all following on; syndicate opens strategic doors |
| 4 | Strong lead with relevant track record; good co-investors; adequate follow-on capacity; some strategic value |
| 3 | Competent lead; adequate syndicate; follow-on capacity present but not exceptional |
| 2 | Weak lead or no clear lead; limited follow-on capacity; minimal strategic value |
| 1 | No institutional lead; misaligned investors; prior investors not following on; governance risk |

## C2: Category Leadership

**What it measures**: Market position, competitive differentiation, and path to category dominance

> Venture-scale returns require companies that define or dominate their category. Category leaders capture disproportionate market share, command pricing power, attract the best talent, and become acquisition targets for strategic buyers. This dimension evaluates whether the company is on a trajectory to become the reference company in its space — the name customers, analysts, and competitors use as the benchmark.

**Questions to Answer**:
- Is the company creating a new category or capturing an existing one?
- What is the current market position? (share, analyst ranking, mindshare)
- What are the competitive moats: IP, network effects, switching costs, data, brand?
- How far ahead is the company relative to the nearest competitor?
- Does the company have category-defining signals (press, analyst recognition)?
- What would it take for a well-funded competitor to replicate this position?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Market share data or analyst rankings (Gartner, Forrester, G2)
- Competitive win/loss rates
- Customer mindshare surveys or NPS data
- Patent portfolio or proprietary technology
- Network effect metrics (user density, marketplace liquidity)
- Brand recognition and press coverage relative to competitors

**Red Flags**:
- Competing head-to-head with well-funded incumbent without clear differentiation
- No structural moats — differentiation based only on features or pricing
- Category being commoditized by open-source or platform incumbents
- Multiple competitors with similar positioning and traction
- No analyst recognition or customer mindshare despite years in market

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Clear category leader or creator; multiple durable moats; competitors reference this company as the benchmark; analyst recognition; 2+ years ahead of nearest rival |
| 4 | Strong #1 or #2 position; defensible moats forming; clear differentiation; growing mindshare |
| 3 | Competitive position in a crowded market; some differentiation; moats still forming |
| 2 | One of many undifferentiated players; weak or temporary moats; no clear path to leadership |
| 1 | Losing ground to competitors; no moats; commoditized offering; no analyst or customer recognition |

## C3: CAGR (Compound Annual Revenue Growth)

**What it measures**: Revenue growth rate, trajectory, and sustainability relative to stage benchmarks

> Compound annual revenue growth is the fundamental engine of venture returns. At growth stage, the question is not just "is revenue growing?" but "is it growing fast enough, from a large enough base, with sufficient durability to reach venture-scale outcomes?" This dimension evaluates historical CAGR, growth trend direction, driver decomposition, and sustainability — all benchmarked against top-quartile companies at the same stage.

**Questions to Answer**:
- What is the historical revenue CAGR? Over what period?
- Is growth accelerating, steady, or decelerating?
- What are the primary drivers: new customers, expansion, pricing, new products?
- What is the net revenue retention (NRR) rate?
- How does growth compare to top-quartile at this stage?
- What are the constraints on continued growth at this rate?
- What is the forward projection and what underpins it?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Revenue data: current ARR/MRR and 12/24-month priors
- Cohort analysis and NRR data
- Growth driver breakdown (new logo vs expansion vs pricing)
- Sales pipeline and conversion metrics
- Stage-appropriate benchmark data (e.g., Bessemer cloud index)
- Forward guidance and management projections

**Red Flags**:
- Decelerating growth without clear reason or plan to re-accelerate
- Growth driven primarily by pricing increases, not volume
- NRR below 100% (net revenue churn)
- Growth concentrated in a few large customers
- Forward projections significantly above historical trend without new driver
- Revenue quality issues (non-recurring, one-time, services-heavy)

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | CAGR >100% at scale ($10M+ ARR); accelerating growth; NRR >130%; diversified drivers; exceeds top-decile benchmarks |
| 4 | CAGR 60-100% with healthy NRR (>120%); steady or accelerating; meets top-quartile benchmarks |
| 3 | CAGR 30-60%; adequate NRR (100-120%); growth steady but not exceptional for stage |
| 2 | CAGR 15-30%; NRR <100%; growth decelerating; below median for stage |
| 1 | CAGR <15% or negative; severe deceleration; revenue quality concerns |

## C4: Capital Efficiency

**What it measures**: Efficiency of converting invested capital into revenue, growth, and enterprise value

> Capital efficiency measures how well a company converts dollars invested into dollars of revenue and enterprise value. At growth stage, this is the difference between a company that can reach milestones with manageable dilution and one that requires excessive capital, destroying returns through dilution. The burn multiple, capital efficiency ratio, unit economics, and gross margin collectively reveal whether the business model is sound and the growth is efficient.

**Questions to Answer**:
- What is the capital efficiency ratio (ARR / total capital raised)?
- What is the burn multiple (net burn / net new ARR)?
- What are the unit economics: CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC, payback period?
- What is the gross margin and how does it compare to sector best-in-class?
- What is the monthly burn and runway in months?
- How much capital is needed to reach the next major milestone?
- Is the company approaching contribution margin positivity?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Total capital raised to date by round
- Current ARR and ARR/capital ratio
- Burn rate trend (improving or worsening)
- Unit economics data from management
- Gross margin trend and sector comparables
- Cash position, burn, and runway calculations

**Red Flags**:
- Burn multiple above 3x (spending $3+ for every $1 new ARR)
- LTV:CAC ratio below 3:1
- CAC payback period above 24 months
- Gross margin below sector median
- Capital efficiency ratio declining over successive rounds
- Runway below 12 months without clear path to next raise
- No clear path to contribution margin positivity

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Capital efficiency ratio >1x; burn multiple <1x; LTV:CAC >5:1; payback <12 months; gross margin above sector top quartile; 18+ months runway |
| 4 | Capital efficiency ratio 0.5-1x; burn multiple 1-2x; LTV:CAC 3-5:1; payback 12-18 months; healthy gross margin |
| 3 | Capital efficiency ratio 0.25-0.5x; burn multiple 2-3x; LTV:CAC 2-3:1; margins adequate but not exceptional |
| 2 | Capital efficiency ratio <0.25x; burn multiple >3x; LTV:CAC <2:1; payback >24 months; margin concerns |
| 1 | Extremely capital-inefficient; unit economics underwater; insufficient runway; no path to efficiency |

## C5: Colossal Market Size

**What it measures**: TAM magnitude, growth rate, and ability to support venture-scale outcomes

> The market must be large enough that even a modest share capture produces outsized returns. "Colossal" is the bar — not just large, but large enough that the company's realistic market share supports a multi-billion-dollar outcome. This dimension evaluates TAM credibility (methodology and source), SAM/SOM realism, market growth dynamics, and whether the company is expanding the market itself through category creation.

**Questions to Answer**:
- What is the TAM? Is it credibly sourced with clear methodology?
- What is the SAM given current product scope? What is the realistic SOM?
- What is the market CAGR and forecast period?
- Is the company creating new market (expanding TAM) or capturing existing?
- Can realistic market share support a $1B+ outcome?
- What adjacent markets could expand the opportunity?
- How sensitive is the market sizing to key assumptions?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Third-party market research (Gartner, Statista, Grand View Research)
- Bottom-up market sizing from customer segmentation
- Comparable company revenue at scale
- Market growth forecast reports with CAGR
- Adjacent market analysis and expansion evidence
- Company's own TAM analysis vs independent verification

**Red Flags**:
- TAM sourced only from company's pitch deck (not independently verified)
- Top-down TAM without bottom-up validation
- Market requires adjacent expansion to support venture-scale outcome
- Market CAGR below 10% (secular stagnation)
- SOM assumptions imply implausible market share
- Market being disrupted by structural shifts (regulation, platform risk)

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | TAM >$50B with credible independent sources; market CAGR >20%; clear SAM/SOM path; category creation dynamics expanding market; realistic share supports $5B+ outcome |
| 4 | TAM $10-50B credibly sourced; CAGR 15-20%; solid SAM/SOM logic; realistic share supports $1-5B outcome |
| 3 | TAM $5-10B; CAGR 10-15%; SAM/SOM reasonable but tight; venture-scale outcome requires strong execution |
| 2 | TAM $1-5B or poorly sourced; CAGR <10%; SOM assumptions aggressive; venture-scale outcome uncertain |
| 1 | TAM <$1B or not credibly sourced; stagnant or declining market; no venture-scale path |

## C6: Counter Cyclicality

**What it measures**: Business resilience to economic downturns, cycles, and external shocks

> The best venture investments produce returns across economic cycles, not just during bull markets. This dimension assesses whether the business model is counter-cyclical (benefits from downturns), recession-resistant (relatively unaffected), or cyclically exposed (vulnerable to downturns). It examines revenue durability, customer stickiness, must-have vs nice-to-have status, and historical or analogous performance through economic stress.

**Questions to Answer**:
- Is the product a "must-have" or "nice-to-have" for customers?
- How would customer demand behave in a recession?
- What percentage of revenue is contractually recurring?
- What is the customer concentration risk?
- How did comparable companies perform through 2020 COVID and 2022 rate-hike cycle?
- Does the company serve sectors that increase spending in downturns?
- Are there regulatory or structural tailwinds independent of economic cycle?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Contract structure analysis (annual vs monthly, cancellation terms)
- Customer concentration data (top 10 customers as % of revenue)
- Comparable company performance through 2020 and 2022 downturns
- Sector cyclicality research
- Customer churn behavior during past stress periods
- Regulatory tailwind analysis

**Red Flags**:
- Majority of revenue is discretionary spend for customers
- High customer concentration (top 3 customers >30% revenue)
- Comparable companies experienced >30% revenue declines in downturns
- Revenue model is transactional (not recurring)
- Product is the first line-item cut in cost reduction
- Company operates in historically cyclical sector without hedging

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Counter-cyclical: business benefits from downturns (compliance, cost-optimization, security); >90% contractually recurring; low customer concentration; proven through prior cycles |
| 4 | Recession-resistant: mission-critical product; >80% recurring; low concentration; comparable performance through downturns |
| 3 | Partially resilient: important but not mission-critical; moderate recurring revenue; some cyclical exposure |
| 2 | Cyclically exposed: discretionary spend; low recurring percentage; high concentration risk; comparable companies suffered in downturns |
| 1 | Highly cyclical: luxury/discretionary; transactional revenue; high concentration; first to be cut in downturn |

## C7: Cash on Cash Return Probability

**What it measures**: Probability-weighted return potential across bull, base, and bear scenarios

> The ultimate question: does the math work? This dimension evaluates the probability and magnitude of cash-on-cash returns across scenarios. It assesses entry valuation reasonableness, exit path credibility, scenario analysis rigor, fund economics fit, and the key risks that could derail the return thesis. A high score requires not just an attractive bull case, but protected downside and a realistic base case that still works for the fund.

**Questions to Answer**:
- Is the entry valuation reasonable relative to stage, traction, and comparables?
- What are the bull/base/bear case return scenarios with probabilities?
- What is the probability-weighted expected cash-on-cash return?
- What are the most likely exit paths? Who are specific acquirers?
- What comparable exits have occurred in this space at what multiples?
- Can this investment return the fund? (fund economics fit)
- What is the expected hold period?
- What dilution risk exists through future rounds?
- What are the top 2-3 risks to the return thesis?

**Evidence Sources**:
- Entry valuation vs comparable company valuations at same stage
- Revenue/ARR multiples for comparable public and private companies
- M&A transactions in sector (acquirers, multiples, deal sizes)
- IPO data for comparable companies
- Fund model and return requirements
- Cap table and dilution modeling
- Management exit timeline expectations

**Red Flags**:
- Entry valuation premium to comparable without clear justification
- Bull case required for acceptable return (base case doesn't work)
- No identifiable acquirers or comparable exits in space
- Expected hold period >7 years
- Dilution through future rounds destroys return even in bull case
- Fund economics don't work (check size too small to matter)
- Single exit path with no alternatives

**Scoring Rubric**:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Attractive entry valuation vs comps; base case returns 5x+; multiple credible exit paths with named acquirers; probability-weighted return >4x; can return the fund; protected downside (bear case >1x) |
| 4 | Reasonable valuation; base case returns 3-5x; credible exit paths; probability-weighted return 2.5-4x; fits fund model; limited downside risk |
| 3 | Fair valuation; base case returns 2-3x; exit paths exist but less certain; probability-weighted return 1.5-2.5x; marginal fund fit |
| 2 | Stretched valuation; base case returns 1-2x; exit paths unclear; probability-weighted return <1.5x; poor fund fit |
| 1 | Overvalued; base case below 1x (loss); no credible exit path; return math doesn't work; fund economics fail |
